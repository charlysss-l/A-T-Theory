Self-Efficacy Theory

According to Albert Bandura, self-efficacy is "the belief in one’s capabilities to organize and execute the courses of action required to manage prospective situations."

According to Bandura, these beliefs influence how people think, act, and feel. The stronger a person’s self-efficacy, the more confident they are in their ability to handle challenges. It determines how you:

  • Set and approach your goals.
  • Feel and behave when facing challenges.
  • Reflect on your successes or failures.

5 Influences of Self-Efficacy

  1. Performance experience refers to an individual's past and relevant experiences, which can shape their belief in their ability to successfully complete a given task. This is often the most significant factor affecting self-efficacy.
  2. Vicarious experience involves observing others—especially those similar to oneself—performing tasks in comparable situations, which can influence one’s own beliefs about their abilities.
  3. Social persuasion refers to verbal feedback—whether encouraging or discouraging—regarding an individual’s capability to perform a task. Generally, the more trustworthy the source, the greater its influence on a person's self-efficacy.
  4. Imaginal experience refers to an individual’s ability to use their imagination to visualize themselves successfully completing a task.
  5. Physical and emotional states pertain to the bodily sensations and emotional reactions a person experiences when approaching a task or challenge.
